Over the past eighteen months, the term "AI psychosis" has migrated from a handful of case reports into the popular press, and from there into the way some clinicians have started to describe patients to me. I want to push back on this. The phrase compresses three things that we should be keeping separate: an exposure (sustained interaction with a particular kind of conversational AI), a clinical phenomenon (delusional or quasi-delusional content that references the AI), and a mechanism (whatever is going on that links the two).

Each of these deserves its own scrutiny, but collapsing them into a single named entity does the opposite. It implies a uniform syndrome, a uniform mechanism, and — most consequentially — a uniform clinical response. None of these are warranted by what we currently know.

## What we actually see

The clinical picture across the case reports varies considerably. In some, a patient with a clear premorbid history of psychotic illness experiences an acute decompensation in which content referencing the AI features prominently. In others, the patient has no such history, the presentation is acute, and the content is florid but resolves rapidly with brief abstinence from the AI. In still others, what is being labeled psychosis looks closer to a fixed and unusual but non-bizarre belief that doesn't generalize beyond the conversational frame.

These are three quite different things, and conflating them under one heading invites three different kinds of clinical error: under-treating the first by attributing it primarily to the AI exposure, over-treating the second by reaching for the antipsychotic when supportive care and abstinence would do, and pathologizing the third when the more parsimonious read is that we are watching the limits of a particular consumer product surface in a measurable way.

## What I'm proposing

Treat "AI psychosis" as a journalistic descriptor, not a clinical one. In clinical documentation, write what you see — the exposure history, the actual symptoms, the mental status — and let the diagnostic framework do the rest. If we find ourselves repeatedly reaching for a single phrase to describe what we are seeing, that is a sign that we need finer distinctions, not a more efficient label.
